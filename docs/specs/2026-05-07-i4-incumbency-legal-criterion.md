# Incumbency as a Redistricting Criterion: Legal Framework

**Series**: I.4
**Status**: Accepted 3.5/4
**Target**: University of Pennsylvania Law Review

## Algorithm / Subject

Legal analysis of incumbency protection as a redistricting criterion: its permissibility, its limits, its interaction with other redistricting criteria, and its relationship to algorithmic redistricting that ignores incumbency entirely. The paper surveys the federal constitutional doctrine on incumbency (permissible but not required), the VRA's interaction with incumbent protection (may conflict when incumbents are from majority-race communities), and state constitutional constraints (some states explicitly permit incumbency consideration; others prohibit using it as the primary criterion). Central argument: \textsc{bisect}'s constitutional silence on incumbency is legally sound, because incumbency protection is a permissible-but-not-required factor whose absence does not make a redistricting process constitutionally defective.

## Key Claims

1. Courts have held that protecting incumbents is a constitutionally permissible redistricting criterion under both state and federal law: \textit{Karcher v.\ Daggett} (1983) dicta identify incumbency protection as a "legitimate objective" that may justify minor population deviations, and \textit{Burdick v.\ Takushi} (1992) recognizes the state interest in election administration stability that incumbency protection serves — but no court has required incumbent protection as a constitutional mandate.
2. Incumbency protection can conflict with the Voting Rights Act when incumbents represent majority-race districts: a mapmaker protecting an Anglo incumbent in a district with a substantial minority population may inadvertently dilute minority voting strength by preserving Anglo-favorable boundaries, creating tension between the permissible incumbency criterion and the VRA's requirements under \textit{Thornburg v.\ Gingles} (1986).
3. \textsc{Bisect}'s constitutional silence on incumbency — the algorithm neither protects nor targets incumbents — is legally sound as a redistricting criterion: algorithmic indifference to incumbency is no different in constitutional effect from the indifference of a neutral commission instructed to apply only population equality and compactness criteria, both of which have been judicially approved as valid redistricting methodologies.

## Legal / Practitioner Value

This paper resolves the most common legal objection to algorithmic redistricting from incumbents and their legislative allies: "The algorithm doesn't consider incumbency, which is a legitimate redistricting factor." The response is threefold: (1) incumbency consideration is permissible, not required; (2) the fact that a legitimate factor is not considered does not invalidate a redistricting plan that satisfies all constitutionally required criteria; and (3) the level of incumbency protection that \textsc{bisect} provides by geographic chance (I.1–I.3) is indistinguishable from neutral redistricting, so the algorithm produces the same outcome that a neutral human mapmaker ignoring incumbency would produce. Courts approving algorithmic redistricting as a remedy or adopting it by statute need this legal foundation.
