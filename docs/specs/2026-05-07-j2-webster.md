# Webster Major Fractions Apportionment

**Series**: J.2
**Status**: Accepted 3.5/4
**Target**: Political Analysis

## Algorithm / Subject

Dedicated treatment of the Webster (major fractions) apportionment method, the principal alternative to Huntington-Hill in the historical U.S. debate. Webster rounds each state's exact quota $q_i = \text{Pop}_i \times H / \text{Total}$ to the nearest integer using standard arithmetic rounding (0.5 threshold), adjusting a common divisor $d$ iteratively until the total seats equals the House size $H$. The method is equivalent to the d'Hondt method's arithmetic mean variant used in Scandinavian electoral systems and is a natural benchmark for evaluating Huntington-Hill's small-state bias. The paper documents Webster's mathematical properties, its historical use in U.S. congressional apportionment (1842, 1911 apportionments were Webster-based), and the \textsc{bisect-apportion} Webster implementation and 2020 hypothetical apportionment.

## Key Claims

1. Webster's arithmetic mean rounding produces seat allocations that minimize the sum of squared deviations between each state's seat share and its population share — a property that makes Webster closer to strict proportionality than Huntington-Hill but potentially less favorable to small states, because Webster's arithmetic mean rounding will more often round down small-state exact quotas than Huntington-Hill's geometric mean rounding.
2. Under 2020 Census data, Webster produces seat allocations identical to Huntington-Hill for 49 states and potentially differs for 1–2 states near the rounding threshold; the specific states that would gain or lose a seat depend on which state falls closest to the 0.5 rounding boundary under the common divisor that produces total seats = 435.
3. Webster is immune to the Alabama paradox and the population paradox, sharing this property with all divisor methods, but the specific common divisor that balances to 435 seats differs from Huntington-Hill's divisor, producing different rounding outcomes for states near the threshold.

## Legal / Practitioner Value

Webster apportionment is the mathematical benchmark that most academic analyses use to evaluate Huntington-Hill's small-state bias. For apportionment reform litigation and policy analysis, J.2 provides the counterfactual: which states would have received more or fewer seats if Congress had chosen Webster instead of Huntington-Hill in 1941. The answer for 2020 data shows the small-state bias of Huntington-Hill quantitatively. For \textsc{bisect-apportion} users, the Webster implementation provides an alternative apportionment step for state legislative redistricting in states that use Webster or for comparative analysis.
