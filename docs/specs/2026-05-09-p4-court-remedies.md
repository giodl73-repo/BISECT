## Claims
1. Courts have ordered "special master" redistricting plans as remedies in at least 12 states since 2010. The special master process is ad hoc, slow, and frequently criticized for lack of neutrality.
2. An algorithmic remedy order — directing the state to run bisect and submit the output as the remedial plan — is both legally permissible and administratively faster than special master appointment.
3. Three model court orders are drafted: (a) mandatory algorithmic remedy, (b) algorithm as special master input, (c) algorithm as adequacy standard (any plan within X% of the bisect plan is presumptively adequate).
4. The SHA-256 audit chain (A.4) satisfies the "verifiable and reproducible" standard that courts have used to evaluate special master plans (citing LWV v. Pennsylvania 2018 special master requirements).

## Key Cases
Harkenrider v. Hochul (2022, NY); LWV v. Pennsylvania (2018); Harper v. Hall (2022, NC); Alabama v. Milligan (2023).
