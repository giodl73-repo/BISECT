## Claims
1. The DIA (model statute in docs/legal/) is constitutionally permissible under Congress's Article I, §4 authority to regulate the "time, place, and manner" of elections.
2. The DIA's specification of the ApportionRegions algorithm by name is unusual but not unprecedented: the Equal Employment Opportunity Act specifies statistical tests by name for adverse impact analysis.
3. Four alternative statutory designs are compared: (a) algorithm-specifying (DIA), (b) criteria-only (prohibit partisan data), (c) commission-mandating, (d) court-remedy-specifying. DIA is the strongest but hardest to pass; criteria-only is the weakest but most feasible.
4. Historical precedent: the Apportionment Act of 1941 (mandating Huntington-Hill) shows that Congress can mandate specific apportionment algorithms. The DIA extends this logic from interstate to intrastate apportionment.
