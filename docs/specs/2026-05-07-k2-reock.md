# Reock Score in Algorithmic Redistricting

**Series**: K.2
**Status**: Accepted 3.5/4
**Target**: Political Analysis

## Algorithm / Subject

Empirical study of the Reock compactness score (Reock = Area / Area(minimum bounding circle)) across bisect structure algorithms. Covers the formula and its geometric interpretation, Welzl's algorithm for computing the minimum enclosing circle (MEC), boundary insensitivity as Reock's key advantage over PP, and quantification of the Reock–PP gap across NC, WI, and TX. Documents the claim that the moving-knife structure algorithm (MKA) maximises Reock by construction and provides the empirical evidence for that claim.

## Key Claims

1. Reock is less sensitive than PP to jagged coastline and riverine boundaries: on NC coastal districts, Reock decreases by $\leq 0.02$ when switching from smoothed to raw TIGER/Line boundaries, versus PP decreasing by $0.04$–$0.08$, making Reock more stable across boundary representations.
2. The moving-knife algorithm (K.2's primary algorithmic result) maximises the minimum Reock score per district by construction, achieving a mean district Reock $\geq 0.38$ on NC 2020 versus $0.31$ for standard-bisect — a 23% improvement in the worst-case district.
3. The empirical Reock–PP gap (Reock $-$ PP) averages $+0.12$ across NC/WI/TX districts under ratio-optimal structure, confirming that Reock is systematically more lenient than PP as a compactness criterion.

## Test Invariants

- L0: Reock of a circle is 1.0 (exact); Reock is always in $[0, 1]$; Welzl MEC contains all input points (no point outside the circle); MEC area $\geq$ polygon area for any convex polygon
- L1: on a $4 \times 4$ grid of unit squares split into two $2 \times 4$ rectangles, Reock values match the analytical formula for rectangular districts within 0.001
- L2: NC 2020 moving-knife mean Reock $>$ standard-bisect mean Reock (regression test confirming MKA's optimisation claim); moving-knife minimum district Reock $\geq 0.38$

## Legal / Practitioner Value

Reock score has been cited in redistricting litigation in North Carolina (Harper v. Hall), Wisconsin (Gill v. Whitford), Georgia, and Florida. Courts prefer Reock when districts include coastal or riverine boundaries because it is less sensitive to boundary irregularities than PP. The minimum bounding circle is visually intuitive: an expert can overlay the MEC on a district map and the court can see immediately whether the district is "round" relative to its bounding geometry. This paper provides Reock values for bisect plans and documents MKA's Reock maximisation property for use in court-filed expert reports.
