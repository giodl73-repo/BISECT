# Adams Smallest Divisors Apportionment

**Series**: J.3
**Status**: Accepted 3.5/4
**Target**: Political Analysis

## Algorithm / Subject

Dedicated treatment of the Adams (smallest divisors) apportionment method, the most small-state-favorable of the five major methods. Adams assigns each state the ceiling of its exact quota $q_i$ divided by a common divisor $d$: each state receives at least $\lceil \text{Pop}_i / d \rceil$ seats, where $d$ is adjusted until the total equals $H = 435$. Because ceiling rounding always rounds up, Adams systematically over-represents small states relative to their population share. The paper documents Adams's mathematical properties, its theoretical position as the extreme of the small-state-favoring spectrum (the opposite extreme is Jefferson, which maximally favors large states), its immunity to paradoxes as a divisor method, and the \textsc{bisect-apportion} Adams implementation.

## Key Claims

1. Adams is the most small-state-favorable divisor method: under Adams, every state receives at least the ceiling of its exact quota, guaranteeing that no state receives fewer seats than it would under strict proportionality — a property that is formally equivalent to maximizing the minimum per-capita representation across all states.
2. Under 2020 Census data, Adams produces substantially different seat allocations from Huntington-Hill for states with small populations: states with 1–3 seats under Huntington-Hill may receive 1 additional seat under Adams, while large states (CA, TX, FL, NY) correspondingly receive fewer seats, because the total must remain 435.
3. Adams is immune to all three classical apportionment paradoxes (Alabama, population, new-states) because it is a divisor method — a formal proof of this immunity, parallel to the proof for Huntington-Hill in J.1, is the paper's main theoretical contribution.

## Legal / Practitioner Value

Adams apportionment is primarily relevant to comparative analysis: understanding where Huntington-Hill sits on the spectrum from most-small-state-favorable (Adams) to most-large-state-favorable (Jefferson) helps contextualize SCOTUS's holding in \textit{Montana} that Congress has broad discretion in choosing among methods. For state legislative apportionment reform, Adams provides the reference point for maximum small-state protection. J.3 also provides the paradox immunity proof that J.5 builds on when contrasting divisor methods with Hamilton's quota method.
