## Research Question

The Census Bureau counts college students at their campus address (usual residence rule), not their home. This inflates college-town districts and deflates home districts. How large is the effect, and does it have a partisan dimension?

## Claims

1. ~8.6 million college students are counted at campus addresses in 2020. College-town districts (e.g., Ithaca NY, Ann Arbor MI, Champaign-Urbana IL) are inflated by 10–35% compared to their non-student population.
2. College towns are systematically Democratic (students skew Democratic). Counting students at campus inflates Democratic-leaning college-town districts at the expense of their home districts, which are often suburban-Republican.
3. The partisan effect is measurable but smaller than the prison gerrymandering effect: approximately 180 tracts nationally would change assignment under a home-address adjustment.
4. Several states (Maine, New York) have ongoing litigation about student counting; the Census Bureau's usual residence rule is not constitutionally mandated.

## Data Sources

- IPEDS (Integrated Postsecondary Education Data System): enrollment by campus
- Census Bureau: group quarters population by type (college/university)
- ACS: residence 1 year ago (proxy for home address)
