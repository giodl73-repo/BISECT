## Claims
1. The primary barrier to redistricting reform is incumbent self-interest: state legislators who control redistricting have a strong incentive to maintain the system that elected them.
2. Reform succeeds through three channels that bypass incumbents: (a) direct democracy (ballot initiatives), (b) judicial mandate (court orders), and (c) federal preemption (federal statute). Commission reform succeeds only when combined with one of these.
3. A political economy model shows that reform probability increases with: (a) partisan competitiveness of the state (neither party dominant), (b) presence of ballot initiative mechanism, (c) recent high-profile gerrymandering litigation.
4. States that have reformed redistricting show measurable improvement in competitive district fraction within 2 election cycles.
