# Review Synthesis — I.3: Open-Seat Effects and Retirement Incentives
**Round**: 1 | **Score**: 2.6/4 | **Verdict**: Conditional Accept (borderline)

## Panel Scores
Jacobson 3/4, Pildes 2/4, Rodden 3/4, Stephanopoulos 2/4, Duchin 3/4

## Consensus Strengths
The open-seat creation mechanism is clearly explained. The three-state analysis provides concrete examples. The retirement incentive argument — that incumbents facing unfamiliar constituent bases may retire rather than seek reelection — is plausible and novel.

## P1 Items (blocking)
- **P1-A** (Stephanopoulos, Pildes): The retirement incentive hypothesis is presented as a finding but has no empirical validation. The paper should either (a) test this using historical redistricting cycles and retirement rates, or (b) explicitly label it as a conjecture with a plan for validation.
- **P1-B** (Pildes): "Open seat" is defined as a district with no incumbent after redistricting — but this conflates districts where incumbents were paired (one must leave) with districts where both incumbents chose new districts. These are different phenomena with different political dynamics. Separate these cases.
- **P1-C** (Jacobson): Single-seed limitation in abstract.

## P2 Items
- Historical validation using post-2010 and post-2020 retirement data would convert the hypothesis to a finding. (Jacobson)
- Cross-reference I.0's open-seat count discussion (NC 4 vs. [0,3] CI). (Rodden)
