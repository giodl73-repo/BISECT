# Review — I.0: Incumbency and Algorithmic Redistricting: Overview
**Reviewer**: Richard Pildes (Election law, redistricting doctrine)
**Round**: 1
**Score**: 3/4
**Verdict**: Minor Revision

## Summary
The legal framework is correctly stated: courts have not recognized "incumbency protection" as a legitimate redistricting criterion under federal doctrine, while incumbent pairing is a recognized harm in some state constitutional litigation. The paper's core legal contribution — establishing that algorithmic redistricting's incumbency-blindness is a legal virtue, not a defect — is sound. The main gap is the absence of citation to the most relevant cases.

## Strengths
- The core legal argument is correct: because bisect cannot see incumbents, it cannot "target" incumbents within the meaning of cases like Rucho and related state precedents.
- The distinction between algorithmic pairing (random, within neutral range) and enacted non-pairing (deliberate protection) is legally significant — it suggests enacted maps are using information that neutral mapmaking would exclude.

## Concerns
- **Missing key cases**: *League of United Latin American Citizens v. Perry* (2006, SCOTUS) directly addresses incumbent protection as a redistricting factor. *Rucho v. Common Cause* (2019) addresses what partisan criteria can be used. Neither is cited.
- **State court variation**: The legal framework varies substantially by state. In Ohio, incumbency is an explicit statutory criterion. In California, it is prohibited. The paper should acknowledge this variation rather than speaking of "the legal framework" as monolithic.
- **Callais relevance**: Post-Callais disentanglement applies to racial motivation; does a similar framework apply to incumbency motivation? The paper should at minimum flag whether incumbency-based mapmaking faces analogous disentanglement challenges.

## Required Changes
- **P1**: Add LULAC v. Perry and Rucho v. Common Cause to the legal section.
- **P1**: Add a paragraph on state-level variation in incumbency as a redistricting criterion.
- **P2**: Note whether Callais-style disentanglement is applicable to incumbency-motivated redistricting.
