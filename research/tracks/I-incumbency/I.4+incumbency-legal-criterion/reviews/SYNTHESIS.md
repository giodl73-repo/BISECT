# Review Synthesis — I.4: Incumbency Protection as a Legal Redistricting Criterion
**Round**: 1 | **Score**: 3.2/4 | **Verdict**: Conditional Accept

## Panel Scores
Jacobson 3/4, Pildes 4/4, Rodden 3/4, Stephanopoulos 3/4, Duchin 3/4

## Consensus Strengths
The strongest paper in the Track I series. The case law synthesis (Karcher, Cox v. Larios, LULAC v. Perry, Thornburg v. Gingles) is comprehensive and correctly stated. The legal asymmetry — incumbency protection permitted under federal doctrine but not mandated — is clearly distinguished from state-level variation. The post-Callais note is appropriate and timely.

## P1 Items
- **P1-A** (Rodden): The 2019 Rucho decision's impact on incumbency protection claims is understated. Rucho forecloses federal court review of partisan gerrymandering, which affects how incumbency-protection claims are presented in federal vs. state courts. Add one paragraph on the post-Rucho landscape.
- **P1-B** (Jacobson): The state-level variation survey (Ohio explicitly permits incumbency; California prohibits it; Texas uses it in practice) should be expanded to all 50 states or acknowledge the limitation of the three-state focus.

## P2 Items
- Callais disentanglement and incumbency: does Callais logic apply when a mapmaker claims incumbency motivation but the effect is racial? (Pildes says this is worth a paragraph.)
- Add a practical guidance table: "If state statute says X about incumbency, what does that mean for algorithmic redistricting?" (Stephanopoulos)
