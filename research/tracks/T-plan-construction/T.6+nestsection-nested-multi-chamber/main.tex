\begin{abstract}
Congressional, state senate, and state house districts for the same state are
currently drawn by three independent redistricting processes.  This independence
creates structural opportunities for coherent gerrymandering: a partisan actor
who controls all three processes can pack or crack a geographic community at
every legislative level simultaneously.  \emph{NestSection} addresses this
opportunity by asking the three chambers to share the largest hierarchical
bisection prefix that their seat counts permit---the \emph{compatible
factorization spine}.

The spine is determined by $g = \gcd(C, S, H)$, where $C$, $S$, and $H$ are
the congressional, senate, and house seat counts.  When $g$ equals the smallest
chamber size, nesting is exact (score $= 0$); when $g = 1$, the chambers share
only a trivial root (score $\approx 100$).  We apply the
\textsc{SpineCompatibility} score to all 50 US states under the 2020
apportionment, finding 11 states with score 0, of which Oregon and Alabama
are the two substantive multi-region exact cases, and identifying the
structural reasons why 39 states require best-effort Mode~3 analysis.

The central policy argument is that a shared geographic hierarchy is a
process constraint on coherent multi-chamber gerrymandering: a partisan actor
who locks in a congressional spine has fewer degrees of freedom when drawing
state senate and house boundaries against the same trunk.  We discuss statutory
design implications and propose a nestability threshold $g \geq 5$ as a
practical criterion for exact-nesting pilots.  The current BISECT workspace
implements nesting validation and audit support; the full three-chamber
constructive pipeline described here remains a design target.
\end{abstract}
