\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Multi-scale MCMC (\ms{}) improves redistricting chain mixing by alternating
between fine-level tract proposals and coarse-level county proposals, with a
fixed probability $\alpha$ of making a coarse move at each step.
The optimal $\alpha$ varies by state: Texas ($k=38$, 254 counties, 5{,}265
tracts) benefits from $\alpha \approx 0.30$, while Iowa ($k=4$, 99 counties,
825 tracts) benefits from $\alpha \approx 0.15$.
Manual tuning of $\alpha$ across 50 states is impractical.

We introduce \ams{}, which tunes $\alpha$ automatically using
\emph{Robbins-Monro stochastic approximation}~\citep{robbins1951}.
Every $\Delta = 50$ steps the algorithm measures the observed coarse acceptance
rate in the current window; if it exceeds the target (0.30) $\alpha$ is
increased; if below, $\alpha$ is decreased.
The Robbins-Monro step size $\gamma_t = \gamma_0 / t^{3/4}$ satisfies the
classical stochastic-approximation conditions
($\sum \gamma_t = \infty$, $\sum \gamma_t^2 < \infty$), but the resulting
trace should be interpreted as a calibrated tuning path rather than a proof of
statewide optimality.
The checked-in \texttt{U.5+adaptive-multiscale-smoke} package verifies alpha
trace replay, clipping, coarse tolerance, and the shared \texttt{MSC\_STEP\_}
seed formula on a synthetic acceptance ledger.

The empirical claim to archive is whether North Carolina ($k=14$), Texas
($k=38$), and Iowa ($k=4$) stabilize at distinct $\alpha$ values and whether
lag-100 edge-cut autocorrelation matches or improves on fixed-$\alpha$ \ms{}.
\ams{} has a Rust substrate in \texttt{bisect-multiscale}; production CLI claims
need the corresponding checked-in command and package path.
\end{abstract}
