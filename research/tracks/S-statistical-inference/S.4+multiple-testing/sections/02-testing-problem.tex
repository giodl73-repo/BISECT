\subsection{Expected False Positives at Each Scale}

Table~\ref{tab:fp-scale} shows the expected number of false positives
at each level of the analysis, assuming the null hypothesis is true
for all tests (no state has a partisan gerrymander) and $\alpha = 0.05$.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Expected false positives at different levels of the L-track analysis.}
\label{tab:fp-scale}
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
Level & Tests & $\alpha$ & E[FP] & FWER \\
\midrule
Single state, single metric & 1 & 0.05 & 0.05 & 5\% \\
Single state, 7 metrics & 7 & 0.05 & 0.35 & 30\% \\
Single cycle, 43 states, 7 metrics & 301 & 0.05 & 15.1 & $>99\%$ \\
All 3 cycles, 50 states, 7 metrics & 1,050 & 0.05 & 52.5 & $>99\%$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The FWER (probability of at least one false positive) exceeds 99\% for
any analysis involving more than a few states.
This means that any claim of ``at least one significant finding'' in a
50-state, 7-metric, 3-cycle analysis is virtually guaranteed to include
at least one false positive---regardless of whether any state has
actually been gerrymandered.

\subsection{The Type I / Type II Tradeoff}

Uncorrected testing at $\alpha = 0.05$ has high Type I error (false positives).
The obvious correction---Bonferroni, which requires $p < 0.05/1{,}050 = 0.0000476$
for significance---has very low Type I error but also very low power.
A state at the 0.3rd percentile ($p = 0.003$) would not survive
Bonferroni correction (which requires $p < 0.000048$).

The Benjamini-Hochberg FDR procedure occupies the middle ground:
it controls the expected fraction of significant findings that are false
positives (at level $q$) while maintaining substantially more power
than Bonferroni.
