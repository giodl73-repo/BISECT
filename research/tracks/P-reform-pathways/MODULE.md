# Track P — Reform Pathways and Implementation

**Theme**: The program proves bisect works as a redistricting algorithm. Track P asks: how does it get adopted? What are the political, legal, and administrative barriers? What does the implementation actually look like in practice — in a court order, a state commission, a federal statute?

**Core question per paper**: How does algorithmic redistricting get from research to law?

## Track Chain

P.0 (why reform is hard) → P.1 (federal legislation) → P.2 (state ballot initiatives)
                         → P.3 (commission integration) → P.4 (court remedies) → P.5 (costs and administration)

## Contracts

Every P.1–P.5 paper must:
- Cite at least one real-world precedent or analogue (existing algorithm use, commission design, court order)
- Provide a concrete implementation template (statute language, commission rule, court order language)
- Identify the primary political barrier to adoption in the described pathway
- Propose a mitigation for that barrier

## Key Real-World Anchors

- **P.1**: DIA (Districting Integrity Act) — the model federal statute in docs/legal/
- **P.2**: California Prop 11/20 (commission with neutrality criteria); Colorado Amendments Y/Z; Michigan Prop 2 (2018)
- **P.3**: California Citizens Redistricting Commission process; Iowa's nonpartisan legislative staff model
- **P.4**: *Rucho v. Common Cause* (2019) — forecloses federal partisan challenges; state courts remain open; *Harkenrider v. Hochul* (2022, NY); *League of Women Voters v. Pennsylvania* (2018)
- **P.5**: Iowa model — fastest and cheapest; California commission — most expensive

## Track Thesis

The political barrier to adoption is not technical (the algorithm works) or legal (the courts have approved algorithmic plans) — it is *incumbency interest*. P.0 establishes this analytically. P.1–P.5 each show a pathway that routes around incumbency by changing who draws the map (commission), changing who mandates it (courts, federal statute, voters directly).
