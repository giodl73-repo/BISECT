## Module Score: 8.4/10

## Track-Level Strengths
- P.4 is the highest-scored paper in the entire N–S program: model court orders are a genuine practitioner contribution that no prior redistricting paper has provided. The three-format structure (mandatory, special-master-input, adequacy-standard) covers all judicial contexts.
- P.1's constitutional authority analysis (Article I §4 + Apportionment Act 1941 analogy) is airtight. The DIA's constitutionality argument is the most legally robust in the program.
- P.5's $50K vs. $3M comparison is the most immediately persuasive number in the program for a legislative audience. Cost is the most credible argument for adoption.
- P.2's model ballot initiative language is immediately deployable — a citizen group could take P.2 §4 language and file an initiative.

## P1 Items by Paper
- **P.0**: The political economy model predicts reform probability but doesn't explain the 2018 MI/CO wave — both passed in states that aren't obviously more competitive than average. Add a "wave election" parameter or acknowledge model limitations.
- **P.3**: The commission adjustment protocol (Steps 1–5) requires a written Gingles justification for each departure — but what standard determines sufficiency? A gerrymandering court would need to know the standard of review.

## P2 Items
- P.1: Add a 50-state survey of constitutional authority for state legislative algorithmic redistricting (as opposed to congressional) — P.1 focuses on congressional; state legislative authority is different and important. (Pildes)
- P.4: Note that Louisiana v. Callais (2026) is the most recent high-profile example of court-ordered redistricting; analyze whether the algorithmic remedy order would have been appropriate there. (Stephanopoulos)
- P.5: The Iowa cost figure ($3M/cycle) needs a primary source citation — currently it's stated without attribution. (Liang)
