# R1 Review — Nicholas Stephanopoulos
**Paper**: B.16 ConvergenceSweep: T=600 Statutory Seed Formula
**Score**: 3.5/4
**Verdict**: Minor Revision

## Summary
B.16 establishes T=600 as the statutory stopping criterion for ConvergenceSweep, provides the SHA-256 content-derived seed formula, and supplies complete proposed statutory language for the Districting Integrity Act. The paper's legal contributions — the Certificate of Seed Neutrality and the correct identification of T=500's certification failure — are the strongest procedural safeguards in the DIA package. As a legal scholar focused on redistricting doctrine, I find this paper close to publication-ready, with three statutory drafting issues that should be addressed before the paper is cited in litigation.

## Strengths
- The Certificate of Seed Neutrality (Section 4.4) is a genuine litigation artifact. The structured affidavit paragraph — "This value was not known to any party until the Census Bureau published the dataset identifier... No human selected this seed... The partisan consequences of this seed were not knowable before that date" — is exactly what a court or special master would need to confirm that the redistricting seed was not politically chosen. No prior algorithmic redistricting proposal has been able to make this certificate.
- The analysis of the Georgia T=500 failure mode correctly identifies the legal problem: T=500 returns the correct plan by luck, but the algorithm cannot certify it because it halted 11 seeds before completing the non-improving run. Courts require certification procedures, not expert opinions that a method was "probably correct." T=600 closes this gap with an 89-seed margin above the observed worst case.
- The proposed statutory text in Section 5.4 is complete: it supplies language for §2a(c)(2)(A) (seed formula), (B) (convergence criterion), and (C) (threshold enforcement). The clause that "values below 600 may be used for research and audit purposes but shall not be used to generate maps with legal effect" is the correct enforcement mechanism — it allows research without undermining the statutory certificate.
- The statutory recommendation to recommend T_stat=600 over T_stat=512 (the minimum sufficient for Georgia) reflects appropriate legal conservatism: the 89-seed margin provides insurance against 2030 census graph changes that might push Georgia's tail above 512.

## Concerns
1. **Section 5.4 clause (C) does not specify an administrative process to raise T_stat.** The conclusion (Section 6.1, "The 2030 census" open question) states that "the statute provides for an administrative process to raise T_stat without full legislative amendment." But Section 5.4 clause (C) does not actually establish this process — it says the reference implementation shall expose a --convergence-threshold flag and that values below 600 shall not generate legally effective maps, but it does not specify how T_stat can be raised administratively if the 2030 census produces a state with a tail exceeding 600. This is a real legislative gap. The statute needs either (a) a clause authorising the EAC to raise T_stat by regulation subject to a public comment period, or (b) a clause requiring full congressional action to raise T_stat, with an interim provision for states whose tails exceed 600 in the interim.
2. **The version-string versioning problem creates an ambiguity for multi-cycle litigation.** Section 4.3 correctly argues that "DIA_SEED_V1" provides domain separation and notes that a future DIA amendment would use "DIA_SEED_V2." But the paper does not specify whether the version string is pinned to the census cycle or to the statute version. If Congress amends the DIA in 2031 to use V2, two different maps could both be "DIA-compliant" for the same 2020 census data under different statute versions. This matters in redistricting litigation: a 2032 challenge to a 2021 V1 map might argue that V2 should have been used. The statutory text should specify that the version string in effect at the time of the census release is binding for the entire redistricting cycle, regardless of subsequent amendments.
3. **The "24-hour statutory window" reference in Section 2.4 is inconsistent with the 30-day submission deadline.** Section 2.4 states the Texas sweep takes "approximately 8.1 minutes — well within the statutory 24-hour window." If the DIA specifies a 30-day submission deadline (standard redistricting statutes), the "24-hour window" comparison addresses the wrong constraint. If the DIA has a 24-hour computation sub-requirement within the 30-day deadline (which would be unusual but defensible for a federal algorithmic mandate), this sub-requirement should appear in the statutory text in Section 5.4.
4. **The litigation posture in Section 4.4 does not address what happens if an opponent runs the sweep and finds a lower EC_norm.** The Certificate of Seed Neutrality invites opponents to "run the sweep and show us the lower edge cut." The paper implies this is impossible under P(tau > 600) < 0.001, but it should specify the legal procedure if it nevertheless occurs — for example, what agency or court decides whether the lower-EC_norm plan found by the opponent is a certified alternative that must replace the statutory map.

## Required Changes
- **P1**: Add statutory language to Section 5.4 clause (C) specifying how T_stat can be raised after the 2030 census sweep, whether by EAC regulation or congressional action.
- **P1**: Specify in Section 4.3 and Section 5.4 that the version string is bound to the census cycle (the version in effect at data release governs the full cycle), preventing multi-cycle ambiguity.
- **P1**: Resolve the "24-hour window" reference: either add this sub-requirement to the statutory text or remove the comparison and reference only the 30-day deadline.
- **P2**: Add a sentence to the Certificate of Seed Neutrality section (4.4) specifying the legal procedure if an opponent runs the sweep and claims to have found a lower EC_norm plan.
