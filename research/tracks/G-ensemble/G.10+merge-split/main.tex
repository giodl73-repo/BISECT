\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Standard ReCom~\citep{deford2021} samples a random spanning tree of a merged
two-district region and selects a balanced cut, then accepts every feasible
proposal.  The resulting chain is fast, but its finite-state target is not
explicitly computed.
Merge-Split MCMC~\citep{janson2022} adds an \emph{independent} reverse
spanning tree and accepts with
$\min(1,\mathrm{valid\_cuts\_fwd}/\mathrm{valid\_cuts\_rev})$.
The ratio corrects the main balanced-cut-count asymmetry: if the forward tree
offered many cuts and the reverse tree offered few, the proposed move was easier
to generate forward than backward and is downweighted.
In BISECT, this is implemented as \texttt{MergeSplitChain} in
\texttt{bisect-ensemble} and exposed through
\texttt{bisect state --search merge-split}.
The implementation is a declared two-tree stochastic correction, not a claim of
exact uniform plan-space sampling: it omits determinant and district-pair-count
corrections and should be audited as an approximate reversible-ReCom chain.
For production search, the CLI records the initial plan and accepted states,
sorts them by edge cut, and returns the requested edge-cut percentile.
Empirical smoke results on North Carolina ($k=14$) and Wisconsin ($k=8$) show
acceptance around~60\%, with plan quality comparable to the neighboring
ForestReCom and standard-ReCom baselines under the same short-run caveats.
\end{abstract}
