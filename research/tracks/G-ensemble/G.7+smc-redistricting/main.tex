\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Sequential Monte Carlo (\smc{}) produces a calibrated weighted sample from the
space of valid redistricting plans.
Unlike \recom{} --- a Markov chain with unknown stationary distribution ---
\smc{} importance-weights each plan by the product of the proposal densities at
each construction stage, giving an asymptotically consistent estimator for the
declared target distribution without Markov chain mixing assumptions.
We implement \smc{} in the \texttt{bisect-smc} Rust crate,
Rayon-parallelised per stage) as \texttt{bisect ensemble --method smc}.
The key algorithmic innovations are: (1)~importance weights equal to
$\log|\mathrm{valid\_cuts}(T)|$, which corrects for the spanning-tree
proposal's non-uniformity; and (2)~largest-component seeding, which preserves
the connectivity invariant that the unassigned subgraph remains connected after
each district assignment.
For inferential claims about the redistricting plan space --- ``what fraction
of valid NC plans have at least 7 Democratic seats?'' --- \smc{} provides a
calibrated alternative to Markov-chain ensembles whose mixing must be
demonstrated separately.
We derive the weight-correctness guarantee (Proposition~1), the connectivity
invariant (Proposition~2), and a first-order \ess{} degradation approximation
that drives the particle-count guidance.
\end{abstract}
