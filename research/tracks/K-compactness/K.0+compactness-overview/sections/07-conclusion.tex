Compactness is not one concept but six. Area-based metrics (Reock, CH) correlate
strongly within their family ($r = 0.79$) but only moderately with perimeter-based
metrics ($r = 0.58$--$0.71$), confirming that no single metric captures all
legally relevant dimensions of shape. PWC is largely orthogonal to geometric metrics
($r < 0.4$), measuring the representational quality of a district rather than
its visual shape.

Courts have recognised this plurality. At least four metrics appear across
redistricting case law---PP, Reock, CH, and Schwartzberg-equivalent---and
no federal court has established a single authoritative metric.
The decision table in Section~5 provides a principled basis for metric selection
by use case, while K.7 argues that a composite six-metric profile is the most
legally defensible approach because it is robust to opposing counsel's
metric-selection arguments.

The \textsc{bisect} platform generates all six metrics simultaneously via
\texttt{bisect label-analyze --types all}, producing a certified JSON appendix
for court filings. All results in K.0--K.7 use 2020 Census TIGER/Line data
with Albers Equal Area Conic projection (EPSG:5070) and single seed $= 42^\dagger$.

Future work in the L track (partisan fairness, L.0--L.6) will examine
whether compactness and partisan metrics trade off, and whether the
algorithm choice that maximises compactness (ratio-optimal for PP, moving-knife
for Reock) systematically favours either party in NC, WI, and TX.
