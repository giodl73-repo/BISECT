The Reock score provides an area-based compactness measure that complements
Polsby-Popper in redistricting litigation.  This paper separates canonical
exact-MBC Reock from the \textsc{bisect} centroid-radius compactness proxy and
from the moving-knife point-cloud search proxy.
Our principal findings are:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item The reported Reock proxy decreases by only $0.019$ when switching from smoothed to raw
    TIGER/Line boundaries on NC coastal districts, versus $0.048$ for PP---a
    2.5$\times$ advantage in boundary stability.
  \item The moving-knife algorithm achieves mean reported Reock
    $\geq 0.38^\dagger$ on NC 2020, a 23\% improvement over standard-bisect in
    this single-seed fixture, supporting but not proving its compactness-search
    design objective.
  \item The empirical Reock--PP gap averages $+0.15^\dagger$ on NC 2020
    under ratio-optimal, showing that the reported Reock proxy is more lenient
    than PP in this fixture.
\end{enumerate}

Practitioners facing coastal-boundary challenges should present Reock as one
area-based metric in a composite profile, supported by the
centroid-plus-max-radius disclosure in Section~3 and the boundary sensitivity
analysis in Section~4.  If exact-MBC Reock is required for cross-software
reproduction, the current \textsc{bisect} compactness report should be disclosed
as an approximation rather than cited as exact Welzl output.

The K.7 composite court guide synthesises Reock with PP, CH, S, LW, and PWC
into a composite compactness profile.
