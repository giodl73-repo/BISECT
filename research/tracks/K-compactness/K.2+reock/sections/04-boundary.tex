We quantify Reock's boundary insensitivity by comparing Reock values computed
on smoothed vs.\ raw TIGER/Line district geometries.

\subsection{Boundary Representations}

\paragraph{Raw TIGER/Line.}
The 2020 Census TIGER/Line tract polygons represent coastlines, rivers, and
state boundaries with the full digitisation detail of the source data.
NC coastal tracts may have $> 1{,}000$ vertices along a single coastal segment.

\paragraph{Smoothed boundaries.}
We apply Douglas-Peucker simplification~\citep{douglas1973algorithms}
with tolerance $\epsilon = 100$ metres to reduce vertex counts by approximately
60--80\% for coastal tracts, while preserving the overall shape of inland boundaries.
This represents the ``true'' compactness of a district stripped of digitisation noise.

The Reock decrease ($-0.019$) is 2.5$\times$ smaller than the PP decrease ($-0.048$).
This confirms Claim~1: Reock is less sensitive to boundary representation
than PP for coastal and riverine districts.

\subsection{Mechanism}

The MBC is determined by at most three extreme points on the district boundary
(by Welzl's algorithm, the boundary set $|R| \leq 3$).
For a coastal NC district, these extreme points are typically the inland extreme
(a Piedmont tract) and two coastal extremes (the northernmost and southernmost
shoreline points), none of which move substantially when digitisation detail is reduced.
The jagged coastline between these extreme points does not affect the MBC.

In contrast, PP depends on the total perimeter $P = \sum_i |e_i|$ summed over all
boundary edges. Each additional vertex introduced by TIGER/Line digitisation of
the coastline adds perimeter length, directly increasing $P$ and decreasing PP.
