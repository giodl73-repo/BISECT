The Reock score compares a district's area to a circle enclosing the district,
providing a compactness measure that is substantially less sensitive to boundary
representation artifacts than the Polsby-Popper score.
Canonical Reock uses the true minimum enclosing circle; the current
\textsc{bisect} compactness report intentionally uses the
centroid-plus-maximum-boundary-radius approximation inherited from the archived
Python pipeline, while the moving-knife structure search uses a separate
projected-centroid point-cloud proxy.
We study reported Reock values across four \textsc{bisect} structure algorithms---standard-bisect,
prime-factor, ratio-optimal, and moving-knife---for North Carolina ($k=14$),
Wisconsin ($k=8$), and Texas ($k=38$) under 2020 Census data.
Key findings: (1) The reported Reock proxy decreases by $\leq 0.02$ when switching from smoothed to
raw TIGER/Line boundaries on NC coastal districts, versus PP decreasing by
$0.04$--$0.08$, supporting the proxy's boundary-insensitivity claim.
(2) The moving-knife algorithm (MKA), an implemented directional sweep that
scores candidate prefixes by minimum point-cloud Reock proxy, achieves mean reported
Reock $\geq 0.38^\dagger$ on NC 2020 versus $0.31^\dagger$ for standard-bisect---a
23\% empirical improvement in mean Reock.
(3) The empirical reported-Reock--PP gap averages $+0.15$ on NC 2020 under
ratio-optimal, showing metric disagreement in this fixture rather than a
universal Reock-over-PP ordering.
All reported district Reock values use the centroid approximation; gaps may
differ from true MBC-based Reock and should be treated as implementation-parity
evidence, not exact-MBC evidence.
All results use single seed $= 42^\dagger$.
