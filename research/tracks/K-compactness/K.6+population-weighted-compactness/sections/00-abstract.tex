Population-Weighted Compactness (PWC) is a moment-of-inertia metric that measures
the average squared distance of residents from their district centroid,
weighted by tract population.
Unlike area-based and perimeter-based metrics, PWC captures representational quality:
a district where voters live far from the district centre imposes higher coordination
costs on constituents and their representative.
We study PWC across four \textsc{bisect} structure algorithms---standard-bisect,
prime-factor, ratio-optimal, and moving-knife---for North Carolina ($k=14$, 2{,}195 tracts),
Wisconsin ($k=8$, 1{,}406 tracts), and Texas ($k=38$, 5{,}265 tracts)
under 2020 Census data (seed $= 42^\dagger$).
Key findings:
(1) PWC--PP correlation is $r < 0.4$ across all algorithm-state pairs,
confirming that representational and geometric compactness are independent dimensions.
(2) Prime-factor reduces mean PWC 18\% below standard-bisect on NC 2020
due to hierarchical nesting aligning population concentrations with district centroids.
(3) TX exhibits high PWC variance across districts due to concentrated urban centres
(Dallas-Fort Worth, Houston, San Antonio-Austin), while WI exhibits low variance
due to more dispersed population.
All results use single seed $= 42^\dagger$ with tract-level population weights
from the 2020 Census PL 94-171 redistricting file.
