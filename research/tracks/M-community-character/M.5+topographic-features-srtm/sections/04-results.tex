\section{Empirical Results}

\subsection{North Carolina Mountain Districts}

Table~\ref{tab:nc-topo} reports results for North Carolina's five westernmost congressional districts (Districts 10--14 in the 2020 apportionment, corresponding to Appalachian communities west of the Blue Ridge Parkway).

\begin{table}[h]
\centering\small
\caption{NC mountain districts: geographic vs.\ topographic weights. ``Cross-ridge'' = number of districts whose boundary crosses a HUC-8 watershed divide.}
\label{tab:nc-topo}
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
Configuration & Districts & Mean PP & Cross-ridge & Pop.\ balance \\
\midrule
Geographic (baseline)  & 5 & 0.171 & 2 & $\pm$0.4\% \\
Topographic weights    & 5 & 0.164 & 0 & $\pm$0.4\% \\
\midrule
Difference             & — & $-4.1\%$ & $-2$ & 0 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Topographic weights eliminate cross-ridge districts at a cost of 4.1\% compactness. The compactness reduction is expected: the Appalachian valleys are elongated along their north-south axis, and ridge-respecting districts follow this elongation. The 4.1\% cost is well within the range of compactness variation seen across seeds (CV $\approx 4.3\%$ for NC per B.7).

\subsection{Proportionality Impact}

Topographic weights in NC produce a proportionality gap change of $+0.3$ pp relative to the geographic baseline ($-6.5$ pp gap), remaining within sampling noise. Valley-respecting districts correspond well to Republican-leaning Appalachian communities: the 0 cross-ridge maps produce the same 7D/7R statewide delegation as the geographic baseline (B.11).
