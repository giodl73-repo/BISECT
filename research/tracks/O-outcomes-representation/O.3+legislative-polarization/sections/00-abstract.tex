Congressional polarization has increased markedly since 2000, with both parties
moving toward their ideological extremes.
A leading explanation is electoral: safe-seat gerrymandering reduces general-election
accountability, shifting effective elections to partisan primaries and producing
more extreme winners.
We test this hypothesis by examining whether congressional district compactness ---
a proxy for safe-seat creation under gerrymandering --- predicts member DW-NOMINATE
ideological scores.

Using Voteview DW-NOMINATE scores for 5{,}355 House member-congress records
from the 107th--118th Congresses (2001--2024) and Polsby-Popper compactness scores
for all districts, we estimate the relationship between district geometry and
member ideology via OLS with member, state, and cycle fixed effects.

The coefficient on district Polsby-Popper is $\hat\beta = -0.071$ absolute
DW-NOMINATE points per 0.10 PP improvement (95\% CI: $[-0.134, -0.008]$),
significant at 5\%.
Less compact (more gerrymander-typical) districts are associated with more
ideologically extreme members, controlling for district partisan composition.

The counterfactual: replacing enacted gerrymanders in NC, WI, TX, FL, and PA
with \textsc{bisect} algorithmic plans would reduce those states' mean
$|\text{DW-NOMINATE}|$ by approximately 0.053 points --- a 13\% reduction
in ideological extremism from this subset of states.
The projection is labeled preliminary${}^\dagger$; it uses the within-party
DW-NOMINATE--competitiveness slope, not a causal identification strategy.
