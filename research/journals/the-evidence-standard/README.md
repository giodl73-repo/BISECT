# The Evidence Standard

The Evidence Standard is a legal, statistical, and reproducibility journal for litigators, expert witnesses, legislators, administrators, and courts. It stages the evidence question: how to quantify a redistricting claim, how to verify provenance, and how to test adversarial manipulation.

Reading The Evidence Standard, a litigator should come away with a disciplined evidence packet: calibrated statistics rather than raw percentiles, an audit-chain protocol for plan provenance, and an adversarial review of gaming vectors. Any bound, timing claim, or legal admissibility claim remains seed language until source-chain and role review are complete.

The Evidence Standard is the final journal in the arc. A reader who has completed District Studies and The Districting Review arrives here with the problem and tool in view. This journal decides which evidentiary claims are strong enough to support a candidate federal rule or a narrower state/court/commission standard.

## Public Posture

- Courts decide admissibility; the journal can supply an argument and record.
- Use "verification protocol" before "proof" unless a formal proof has passed review.
- Quantitative bounds need scope, data version, method, and uncertainty.
- Adversarial objections should be stated in their strongest form before answering.
