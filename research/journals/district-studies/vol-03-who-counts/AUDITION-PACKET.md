# Audition Packet: Who Counts?

This packet is the current human-review front door for District Studies Vol. 3.
The issue is in **audition**, not provisional public preview and not locked
publication.

## Recommended Strategy

Use the qualitative preview path. Vol. 3 should explain why population equality
depends on the chosen population base, using prison gerrymandering as the
clearest example and citizen VAP as the hard legal case. It should not publish
exact national shift estimates or a final statutory recommendation.

See [Qualitative Preview Path](decisions/qualitative-preview-path.md).

## Reader Promise

Population in redistricting is less settled than it looks. Prison residence,
citizen voting-age population, students, and overseas populations all change
what a map is asked to represent.

## Article Summaries

### 1. Counting Is a Choice

The opening article should make the hidden premise visible: equal population is
not a complete instruction until someone specifies which population count is
being equalized. The article should avoid recommending a rule. Its job is to
prepare the reader for the rest of the issue.

### 2. Prison Gerrymandering Is the Clearest Test

The lead article should explain why counting incarcerated people at facility
locations can move representational weight away from home communities. This is
the strongest public hook because the input choice is concrete, administratively
visible, and directly connected to Census residence rules. The article must not
claim that current maps violate the VRA or that an implementation mode already
exists unless the source chain proves it.

### 3. The Population Menu

This article should define the main candidate population bases: total
population, voting-age population, citizen voting-age population, and adjusted
counts. It should give the reader a clean vocabulary before the issue moves to
legal or empirical consequences.

### 4. Citizen VAP Is the Hard Case

This article should explain why citizen VAP is not just another data column. It
raises legal questions, ACS sampling-error questions, and representational
questions at the same time. The preview version can say the choice changes map
construction incentives, but it must not publish an exact national shift value.

### 5. The 50-State Comparison Question

This article should introduce N.5 as the synthesis candidate and explain what a
complete comparison would need to show: disruption, direction, compactness, data
quality, and coverage across population definitions. It should not call the
table definitive until the coverage gate closes.

### 6. What This Issue Does Not Settle

The guardrail article should be explicit. The issue does not choose a final
statutory population base, does not resolve every state-law question, does not
publish exact national shift claims, and does not treat student or military
counting as solved.
