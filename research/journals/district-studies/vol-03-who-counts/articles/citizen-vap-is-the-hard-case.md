# Citizen VAP Is the Hard Case

Citizen voting-age population is the hard case because it changes three things
at once: the legal question, the data question, and the representation question.

The legal question is not uniform across every redistricting context. A rule for
congressional districts is not automatically the same as a rule for state
legislative districts. Any public discussion of citizen VAP has to keep those
contexts separate.

The data question is also different. Total population comes from the decennial
census redistricting file. Citizen voting-age population commonly depends on
survey-derived estimates. That does not make it unusable for every purpose, but
it does mean uncertainty and aggregation need to be visible before anyone treats
small differences as precise map instructions.

The representation question is the one readers notice first. Switching from
total population to citizen VAP changes which places carry population credit.
Areas with many noncitizen residents would be treated differently than areas
where nearly all residents are adult citizens. That can alter the shape and
political character of districts.

This issue should treat citizen VAP as a boundary case rather than a headline
answer. It is too important to ignore and too sensitive to summarize with a
single number.
