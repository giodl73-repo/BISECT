# Prison Gerrymandering Is the Clearest Test

The clearest way to see the population-base problem is to look at incarcerated
people.

The census counts people where they usually reside. For incarcerated people,
that often means the correctional facility, not the home community. In
redistricting, that facility count becomes part of the district population.
People who cannot vote from the facility location can still increase the
representational weight of the district that contains the facility.

That is why prison gerrymandering works as the public entry point for this
issue. It is concrete. It is not an abstract fight over metrics. The reader can
see the input question: should the map count a person at the facility address or
at the home community?

Different jurisdictions have answered that question differently. Some states
have adopted residence-address corrections for redistricting. Others continue
to use unadjusted census counts. The point for this issue is not to settle every
legal question. The point is that the same map-drawing procedure can produce a
different representation structure when the population input changes.

For algorithmic redistricting, that distinction is essential. A neutral
partitioner balances the weights it is given. If the weights encode a disputed
residence rule, the output will faithfully reflect that rule.
