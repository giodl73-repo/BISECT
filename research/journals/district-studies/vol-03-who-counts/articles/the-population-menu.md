# The Population Menu

Population equality is easier to discuss when the choices are named plainly.

**Total population** counts all residents in the census population base used for
redistricting. It is the default for most redistricting work and the most
administratively stable input.

**Voting-age population** counts residents old enough to vote, whether or not
they are citizens or registered voters. It narrows the represented public but
does not answer citizenship or registration questions.

**Citizen voting-age population** counts adult citizens. It aims closer to the
eligible electorate, but it relies on data with different provenance and
sampling properties than the decennial total-population count.

**Adjusted population** starts from a census base and reallocates or excludes
specific populations. Prison adjustment is the leading example in this issue:
the question is whether incarcerated people should be counted at facility
locations or home communities.

Each option answers a different theory of representation. Total population
emphasizes representation of residents. Citizen voting-age population emphasizes
eligible voters. Adjusted counts emphasize correcting a specific residence-rule
distortion.

The menu matters because each option can be stated in technical language while
carrying legal, civic, and data-quality consequences. A public rule should say
which population it means before it asks a map to be equal.
