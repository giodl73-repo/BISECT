# Geography Constrains Proportionality

The T.5 contribution can be stated without importing unresolved numeric
claims.

The public-safe claim is that proportional partisan outcomes can be
geographically constrained under contiguous single-member districts. Statewide
vote share does not describe how voters are distributed across the map. When
one party's voters are spatially concentrated and the other party's voters are
more dispersed, compact geographic districts may produce asymmetric outcomes
without intentional partisan targeting.

The key distinction is between statewide arithmetic and district geography.
Statewide arithmetic asks how many votes each party received. District
geography asks where those votes are. Single-member districts convert a spatial
distribution into seats. That conversion can make proportionality harder than a
statewide vote share suggests.

T.5 is the formal lead source for this point. It supplies the issue's central
diagnostic idea: geography can set limits before policy choice or legal remedy
enters the argument.

Exact T.5 table values remain out of scope until the source request is closed
for METIS parameters, seed treatment, and C(G) provenance.

## Public Example

Proportionality can sound simple: if a party wins roughly half the statewide
vote, a reader may expect roughly half the seats. Geography complicates that
expectation. If a party's voters are packed into a small number of dense places,
contiguous compact districts may convert those votes into fewer seats than a
statewide percentage alone would suggest.

values until the T.5 source-chain requests are closed.
