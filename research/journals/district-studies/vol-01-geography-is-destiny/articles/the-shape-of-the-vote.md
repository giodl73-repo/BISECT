# The Shape of the Vote

The map line matters. But every map line is drawn over a voter geography that
already has partisan structure.

"Geography Is Destiny" is a title, not a theorem. The narrower claim of this
issue is that partisan outcomes are often strongly constrained by where voters
live. That claim matters because it changes the first question. Before asking
whether a map has been manipulated, the public needs to ask how much of the
outcome is already built into the spatial distribution of voters.

A geography-only baseline can still produce politically consequential outcomes.
That does not make it automatically fair. It makes it inspectable. It gives the
reader a starting point for separating geography, method, legal constraints,
and intentional political choice.

This issue starts with three questions:

1. How much of a partisan outcome can be explained by geography alone?
2. When does a compact geographic baseline converge with the broader plan
   space?
3. What remains after geography is accounted for?

The issue does not argue for adoption. It prepares the reader for diagnostic
evidence before any remedy claim appears.

## Public Example

A reader can picture two states with the same statewide vote split. In one
state, voters from both parties are mixed across many communities. In another,
one party is heavily concentrated in a few urban areas while the other is spread
more evenly. The same compactness-first districting procedure can face very
different feasible outcomes in those two states.

## Publication Boundary

Do not use this piece to claim that geography explains every partisan outcome,
that all asymmetry is natural, or that an algorithmic baseline should be
adopted. Those are later questions.
