# Geography Constrains Proportionality

## Draft Claim

B.12 is the issue's lead formal source because it explains why proportional
partisan outcomes can be hard to achieve under contiguous single-member
districts. The public-safe version is:

> The proportionality problem is partly geographic. A map can follow compact,
> contiguous geography and still produce partisan asymmetry because voters are
> not distributed proportionally across space.

This is not a claim that proportionality is impossible everywhere. It is not a
claim that a non-proportional result proves manipulation. It is a claim that
geography sets constraints that any diagnostic standard has to account for.

## Article Shape

1. Explain the naive expectation: if votes are close to 50/50 statewide, seats
   should be close to 50/50.
2. Explain the geographic objection: statewide vote share is not spatially
   uniform.
3. Introduce the B.12 idea at a high level: proportionality has a geographic
   feasibility condition.
4. Use B.12 as a formal reason to ask for baselines before accusations.
5. Close with the caveat: the paper is useful for diagnosis, not for a legal
   conclusion in this issue.
