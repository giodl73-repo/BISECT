# The Shape of the Vote

## Draft Editorial Frame

The first mistake in talking about gerrymandering is to start with the line on
the map.

The line matters. But the line is drawn over a prior fact: voters are already
distributed unevenly across space. Some voters are concentrated in cities. Some
are spread through suburbs, small towns, and rural areas. A districting plan is
therefore not choosing from a neutral blank page. It is carving a geography that
already contains partisan structure.

This issue starts there.

"Geography Is Destiny" is a title, not a theorem. The claim under review is
narrower and more useful: partisan outcomes are often strongly constrained by
where voters live. If that is true, then some asymmetry in district outcomes can
appear even when a mapmaker is following geographic rules rather than partisan
targets. That does not excuse manipulated maps. It does change what has to be
measured.

The issue asks three questions:

1. How much of a partisan outcome can be explained by geography alone?
2. When does a compact geographic baseline converge with the broader plan
   space?
3. What remains after geography is accounted for?

The first question belongs to T.5, which studies why proportional partisan
outcomes can be hard to achieve under contiguous single-member districts. The
second belongs to G.1, which compares a Bisect plan against ReCom ensemble
runs. The third is a measurement question, and it remains gated until the
efficiency-gap output record is traced to the current sign convention.

The political effect still matters. A geography-only baseline can produce
politically consequential outcomes even when partisan data never enters the
algorithm. That is why this issue treats the baseline as evidence to inspect,
not as a shortcut to calling an outcome fair.

This order matters. Public argument should not begin by demanding a remedy. It
should begin by separating diagnosis from prescription. District Studies Vol. 1
does not ask the reader to adopt an algorithmic redistricting rule. It asks the
reader to inspect a baseline: what happens when a map is generated from
population and geography, before partisan optimization enters the room?

If the baseline produces asymmetry, the next question is not automatically
"who cheated?" It is "how much of this result was already in the geography?"
Only after that question is answered can the remaining claim be made
responsibly.

## Claims This Editorial May Make

- Geography can structure the feasible plan space before any mapmaker acts.
- Compact geographic baselines can help distinguish geography-driven asymmetry
  from claims that require evidence of manipulation.
- Geography-only baselines can still have partisan consequences.
- District Studies Vol. 1 is diagnostic, not an adoption argument.

## Claims This Editorial Must Not Make

- Geography determines all partisan outcomes.
- A geography-only algorithm is legally neutral by construction.
- Any enacted map is unconstitutional because it differs from the baseline.
- The federal rule follows from this issue alone.
