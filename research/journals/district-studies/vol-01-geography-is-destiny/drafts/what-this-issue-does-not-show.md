# What This Issue Does Not Show

## Purpose

This note is the issue's guardrail. It prevents a strong public title from
turning into claims the reviewed evidence does not support.

## Draft Note

This issue studies geography before remedy. That makes its limits important.

It does not show that geography determines every partisan outcome. Geography
can strongly constrain the feasible plan space, but different states, data
sources, and district counts can behave differently.

It does not show that every non-proportional outcome is natural. A geographic
baseline can explain part of an outcome; it does not automatically explain all
of it.

It does not show that any enacted map is unconstitutional. Legal claims require
different evidence: state law, federal doctrine, intent, effects, standards of
review, and remedy authority.

It does not show that a federal rule follows from this issue alone. District
Studies Vol. 1 is one diagnostic step in a larger research program.

It does not publish final efficiency-gap conclusions. The code-level sign
convention has been corrected, but result-bearing measurement outputs need
post-fix provenance before they can carry public numeric claims.

It does not certify ensemble percentiles as court-ready statistics. The G.1
summary uses preliminary ensemble positions with effective-sample-size caveats.

It does not claim that a geography-only baseline is the only possible standard.
It asks what such a baseline reveals before policy choice enters the argument.

## Review Checklist

- BOUNDARY: no constitutional conclusion.
- SCALE: no raw percentile or efficiency-gap number without provenance.
- DATUM: every source limitation has a linked audit note.
- COMMONS: the caveats are plain enough for a public reader.
