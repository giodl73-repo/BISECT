# After the Map

Vol. 1 asks how much voter geography constrains partisan outcomes before any
remedy claim appears. Vol. 2 asks the next question: after a map is drawn, what
changes for voters and representatives?

The issue does not assume that every downstream outcome is proven. It auditions
outcome claims by strength. Some outcomes, like constituent distance, are closer
to direct measurement. Others, like turnout and polarization, require stronger
identification before they can support public claims.

That ordering is the point. A public issue about outcomes should not treat all
metrics as equally mature. It should help the reader see which claims are ready
for inspection, which are plausible but fragile, and which belong in a later
evidence-standard issue.
