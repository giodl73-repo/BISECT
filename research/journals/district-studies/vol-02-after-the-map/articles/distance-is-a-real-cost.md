# Distance Is A Real Cost

Constituent distance is the cleanest Vol. 2 anchor because it is concrete.
District shape affects how far constituents may need to travel to reach a
representative's district office. That is not an abstract democratic theory; it
is an access cost.

The [O4] source builds this claim from district geometry, office-location
assumptions, geocoding, and road-network routing. For preview purposes, the
safe claim is that constituent distance is a computable outcome worth reviewing.

A non-compact district can connect distant communities through narrow corridors.
That structure may preserve a political arrangement while making constituent
access less equal inside the district. A compact district does not guarantee
good representation, but it gives the access question a measurable geographic
form.

This preview does not publish the source's exact travel-time values. The public
lesson is simpler: some map harms are spatial before they are partisan.
